1. B2B is the real revenue, community is the moat. ERA proved individual-access emergency services do not generate sustainable revenue in Nigeria. Siren's community features (resource board, Guardian Mode, Commute Shield) build the user base and trust. But the revenue comes from corporate contracts. Start thinking now about which companies in Lagos would pay for location subscriptions across their employee base. Logistics companies with dispatch riders are the obvious first target — their riders are constantly in the incident zones Siren covers.
2. The cost-per-incident has to be near zero for community users. ERA's ₦173,875 per intervention pricing locked them into a corporate-only viable model. Siren's zero-cost public entry is not just a product decision — it is a structural requirement for Lagos-scale adoption. Never charge the reporter. Never charge the responder. Monetise the organisations and corporates.
3. Responder retention is real and achievable. ERA's 74% responder retention in their pilot is genuinely encouraging. People who sign up to help in emergencies stay committed. Your biggest supply-side challenge is not retention — it is initial sign-up and geographic distribution. Focus your early marketing on recruiting responders in underserved zones (Alimosho, Agege, Eti-Osa) not just the Ikeja/VI axis where ERA concentrates.
4. Cultural resistance is your hardest problem, not the technology. 90% of ERA's pilot patients still went via non-EMS. The technology worked. The culture didn't follow. Your loop closure mechanic — showing the reporter what happened, who helped, how fast — is the correct response to this. Every closed incident is a proof point that changes one person's belief about whether the system works. Make those closure messages as specific and human as possible. "Bola (Nurse, Surulere) was on scene in 4 minutes" is more behaviour-changing than "a responder was dispatched."
5. Their API is a signal worth following. ERA built an API to sell their dispatch capability to other platforms. This is smart infrastructure thinking. Siren should add an incident data API to the Year 2 roadmap — allowing HMOs, logistics platforms, and eventually LASEMA to consume verified incident data. This turns Siren from a consumer product into infrastructure, which is how you scale revenue without scaling headcount.
6. The Edo State government partnership model is replicable. ERA partnered with Edo State government to pilot their responder network with state support. This gave them legitimacy, a funded trial cohort of 170 responders, and government credibility. Lagos State is currently strengthening its command and control centre and recently launched a coastal community first responder programme. Siren should make approaching Lagos State Emergency Management Agency the Year 1 government relations goal — not for operational dependency, but to position as the community data layer that complements LASEMA's official response infrastructure.
